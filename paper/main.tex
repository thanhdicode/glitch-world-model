\begin{abstract}
Gameplay glitches often unfold over time, making sequence-level evidence important for automated
video-based quality assurance. We present a leakage-aware empirical evaluation of latent-surprise
signals derived from artifact-backed LeWorldModel checkpoints. The protocol uses normal-only
training evidence, separates calibration from evaluation before windowing, aggregates window
scores at episode level, and retains source-, pair-, and episode-disjoint roles. On the main frozen
non-locked TempGlitch follow-up split, every method is evaluated on the same 12 normal-negative and
22 buggy-positive episodes after threshold calibration on two normal episodes. The best recorded
LeWM configuration reaches AUROC 0.7159 and AUPRC 0.8026, compared with AUROC 0.6136 and AUPRC
0.7310 for the strongest simple baseline by AUROC. The AUROC confidence intervals overlap,
evaluation support is small, and FPR@95TPR remains 0.7500. A separate R5-XGame result provides
additional bounded non-locked evidence but is not treated as directly comparable. These findings
support further investigation of latent surprise under stronger baselines and broader protocols;
they do not establish broad superiority, cross-game generalization, temporal localization, or
deployment readiness.
\keywords{gameplay glitch detection \and latent surprise \and world models \and leakage-aware evaluation \and reproducibility}
\end{abstract}
