\begin{abstract}
We propose \textbf{LeWM-Glitch}, a label-free gameplay glitch detector that uses the latent
prediction error of a JEPA-style world model trained only on normal gameplay. The method scores
each video window by the distance between predicted and observed latent states, aggregates this
surprise signal to the episode level, and calibrates thresholds using normal-only episodes. We
instantiate LeWM-Glitch from artifact-backed LeWorldModel~\cite{lewm} checkpoints and evaluate it
with source-, pair-, and episode-disjoint roles before windowing.

On the frozen pair-disjoint TempGlitch follow-up (12 normal-negative, 22 buggy-positive
episodes), the best LeWM-Glitch row achieves AUROC 0.7159 and AUPRC 0.8026, above the strongest
CNN-LSTM baseline AUROC 0.6136~\cite{tempglitch}. An auxiliary expanded TempGlitch run increases
the evaluation support to 67 episodes and records AUROC 0.7005. On the frozen R5-XGame split
(12 normal-negative, 60 buggy-positive episodes), LeWM-Glitch achieves AUROC 0.9097 and AUPRC
0.9814, compared with 0.7681 for feature distance and 0.7167 for frame difference. We also report
honest negative findings: LeWM-Glitch reaches only AUROC 0.5 on the image-level GlitchBench K2
split, and K3 finds no measurable SIGReg or real-action benefit. All evidence is non-locked and
split-bounded; we make no claims of broad superiority, cross-game generalization, temporal
localization, or deployment readiness.
\keywords{gameplay glitch detection \and latent world models \and JEPA \and latent prediction error \and leakage-aware evaluation}
\end{abstract}
