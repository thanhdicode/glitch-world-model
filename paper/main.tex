\begin{abstract}
Gameplay glitches often unfold over time, making sequence-level evidence important for automated
video-based quality assurance. We study an artifact-backed JEPA-style latent-surprise detector
derived from LeWorldModel checkpoints under a leakage-aware normal-only protocol that separates
fitting, calibration, and evaluation before windowing. On the main frozen non-locked TempGlitch
pair-disjoint follow-up, every method is evaluated on the same 12 normal-negative and 22
buggy-positive episodes after threshold calibration on two normal episodes. The best recorded
LeWM row reaches AUROC 0.7159 and AUPRC 0.8026. Exact-support learned baselines add a strongest
CNN-LSTM row at AUROC 0.6136, with paired delta-AUROC -0.1023 versus the best LeWM row and a 95\%
bootstrap interval that crosses zero. On the validated bounded image-level K2 GlitchBench split,
\texttt{feature\_distance} and all three learned baselines reach AUROC/AUPRC 1.0 while the best
recorded LeWM row reaches AUROC 0.5, yielding an honest negative comparison surface for the
current LeWM configuration. A controlled K3 validation-normal ablation shows no measured
prediction-loss benefit from enabling SIGReg and no stable real-action-over-zero-action gain.
Window-level surprise timelines are reproducibly generated for qualitative inspection, but
temporal localization remains future work because the validated artifacts do not expose true span
annotations. The evidence is non-locked and split-bounded; it does not establish broad
superiority, cross-game generalization, temporal localization, or deployment readiness.
\keywords{gameplay glitch detection \and latent surprise \and JEPA-style prediction \and leakage-aware evaluation \and reproducibility}
\end{abstract}
