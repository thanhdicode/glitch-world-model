\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Gameplay glitches are visible software failures that can disrupt interaction, presentation, or
progression. Detecting them from gameplay video is attractive because recorded visual evidence is
widely available during quality assurance, yet manual review is expensive, repetitive, and
difficult to scale. The task is also unusually easy to mischaracterize: many glitches are temporal
events whose evidence lies in motion, persistence, or an unexpected transition rather than in a
single screenshot \cite{glitchbench,tempglitch}. Sequence-level scoring is therefore a more
appropriate target for this study than isolated-frame classification.

Latent predictive world models offer one plausible signal. If a model trained on normal gameplay
predicts future latent states, unexpectedly large prediction error may indicate a departure from
learned dynamics. Joint-embedding predictive architectures and latent world models motivate this
idea \cite{ijepa,vjepa,worldmodels,planet,dreamerv3,lewm}, but that motivation is not itself
evidence that latent surprise detects gameplay glitches. Empirical support must come from a
protocol that prevents related episodes from leaking across roles, calibrates without buggy
evaluation examples, and compares methods on identical support.

This paper conducts a leakage-aware empirical evaluation of latent-surprise signals for gameplay
glitch detection. The main evidence is a frozen pair-disjoint, non-locked TempGlitch follow-up in
which two normal episodes define the operating threshold and all methods are evaluated on the same
12 normal-negative and 22 buggy-positive episodes. A separate R5-XGame family provides secondary
bounded evidence on a different frozen distribution. The two families are deliberately not pooled
or treated as proof of cross-game generalization.

The contributions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item a JEPA-style latent-surprise detector formulation, instantiated from artifact-backed LeWM
checkpoints, with source-, pair-, and episode-aware role separation before windowing;
\item an exact-support episode-level comparison of latent surprise against simple and learned video
baselines on the frozen non-locked TempGlitch follow-up;
\item two additional controlled evidence lanes: a bounded public-benchmark negative comparison on
the K2 GlitchBench slice and a K3 mechanistic SIGReg/action ablation on validation-normal
training readouts; and
\item a reproducible paper surface in which tables, qualitative timelines, provenance records, and
claim-registry mappings constrain what the manuscript may and may not say.
\end{itemize}

The scope is intentionally bounded. The study does not claim a universal glitch detector,
state-of-the-art performance, broad method superiority, cross-game generalization, temporal
localization, SIGReg benefit, action-conditioning benefit, or locked-test performance.
