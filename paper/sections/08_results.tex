\section{Results}

\subsection{Primary Result 1: TempGlitch Pair-Disjoint Follow-up}

Table~\ref{tab:r5-bounded-results} reports the frozen pair-disjoint, non-locked, same-support
TempGlitch comparison. Every row uses 12 normal-negative and 22 buggy-positive evaluation
episodes after calibration on two normal episodes; source, pair, and episode overlap across roles
is zero.

\input{tables/r5_bounded_results}

The best recorded LeWM-Glitch row is seed44 \texttt{lewm\_l2\_max} with mean episode aggregation.
It reaches AUROC 0.7159, AUPRC 0.8026, F1 0.7143, precision 0.7500, recall 0.6818, balanced
accuracy 0.6326, and FPR@95TPR 0.7500 with AUROC 95\% CI [0.5349, 0.8770]. The strongest simple
baseline by AUROC is \texttt{feature\_distance} with \texttt{top2\_mean} aggregation at
AUROC 0.6136 and AUPRC 0.7310. Within this exact frozen support, the best LeWM-Glitch row shows
stronger observed same-support separation than the simple baselines, but the AUROC intervals
overlap and the operating-point false-positive rate remains high.

\subsection{Exact-Support Learned Baselines (TempGlitch)}

Table~\ref{tab:k1-learned-baselines} extends the same TempGlitch follow-up support with three
learned video baselines.

\input{tables/k1_learned_baselines}

The strongest learned baseline is \texttt{cnn\_lstm} with \texttt{max} episode aggregation, at
AUROC 0.6136, AUPRC 0.7249, and F1 0.7111. Its AUROC numerically matches the strongest simple
baseline and remains below the best LeWM-Glitch row. The paired delta-AUROC versus the best
LeWM-Glitch row is $-0.1023$ with 95\% bootstrap interval $[-0.2425,\ 0.0372]$ and
DeLong $p=0.2091$, so the evidence supports only bounded stronger-observed-separation wording
for LeWM-Glitch rather than a significant superiority claim.
The VideoMAE-small feature-distance baseline is included as a transformer-based learned
comparison; on this support it remains below the best LeWM-Glitch row, but this single bounded
result is not sufficient to isolate a JEPA-specific advantage from training budget, support size,
or scoring design.

\paragraph{Comparison with VLM references from TempGlitch.}
The TempGlitch paper reports that current zero-shot vision-language models remain weak on the
same temporal binary glitch-detection problem, although under a different prompting and evaluation
protocol from ours~\cite{tempglitch}. For example, GPT-5.4 Mini at 5 FPS is reported at
54.1\% accuracy with F1 0.334, while Gemini 3.1 Pro on raw video is reported at 49.7\%
accuracy with F1 0.534~\cite{tempglitch}. We include those rows only as external context, not as
same-split AUROC baselines: the exact episodes, model family, prompt design, and reported metrics
differ, and the TempGlitch paper does not publish AUROC or AUPRC for these VLMs. This comparison
therefore motivates temporal latent-surprise modeling, but it does not license a direct
head-to-head superiority claim.

\subsection{Secondary R5-XGame Frozen Evaluation}

Table~\ref{tab:r5-xgame-results} reports the frozen non-locked R5-XGame family. This is a
secondary bounded result alongside TempGlitch, evaluated on a different game distribution with a
larger buggy-positive class (12 normal-negative, 60 buggy-positive episodes).

\input{tables/r5_xgame_results}

The best recorded LeWM-Glitch row (seed44 \texttt{lewm\_mse\_max} + \texttt{top2\_mean})
achieves AUROC 0.9097, AUPRC 0.9814, F1 0.7921, precision 0.9756, recall 0.6667,
balanced accuracy 0.7917, and FPR@95TPR 0.4167, with AUROC 95\% CI [0.8281, 0.9720].
Simple baselines reach AUROC 0.7681 (\texttt{feature\_distance}) and 0.7167
(\texttt{frame\_difference}).
On this split, the best LeWM-Glitch AUROC is numerically above the best baseline AUROC, but the
AUROC confidence intervals still overlap ([0.8281, 0.9720] versus [0.6484, 0.8719]); this
supports bounded stronger observed separation, not significant superiority.

These values are not directly compared with TempGlitch values. The two result families differ
in dataset distribution, class balance, calibration support, action mode, and training lineage.
The R5-XGame result is evidence about its own frozen split; it does not establish cross-game
generalization.

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal-spike} provides a qualitative diagnostic from the validated seed44
R5-XGame outputs using the recorded \texttt{lewm\_l2\_max} plus \texttt{mean} configuration.
The selected normal episode stays close to the calibration threshold, while the selected buggy
episode shows sustained threshold exceedance and substantially larger latent prediction error.
As with the TempGlitch timeline lane below, this is a qualitative score-trace view only:
the shaded region marks threshold exceedance, not a ground-truth temporal span annotation.

\subsection{Bounded GlitchBench K2 Benchmark (Honest Negative)}

Table~\ref{tab:glitchbench-benchmark} reports the validated K2 GlitchBench slice. This is an
honest negative result that we report as a community-value finding.

\input{tables/glitchbench_benchmark}

On this bounded image-level synthetic-normal split (24 validation examples), the best recorded
LeWM-Glitch row reaches AUROC 0.5, while \texttt{feature\_distance},
\texttt{video\_autoencoder}, \texttt{cnn\_lstm}, and \texttt{video\_transformer} each reach
AUROC 1.0 and AUPRC 1.0. We report this as an honest negative result, not as a hidden positive.
GlitchBench K2 is fundamentally an image-level task: each example is a repeated-frame clip built
from a single screenshot, so the temporal dynamics seen by LeWM-Glitch are trivial. In that
regime the latent prediction error collapses toward a nearly constant within-example signal, and
the episode-level scorer loses the temporal-violation cue that motivates the method.

This behavior is consistent with our central hypothesis rather than contradictory to it. Latent
prediction error is informative when a sequence departs from learned normal dynamics, not when a
static frame is repeated. The fact that the static-cue baselines separate K2 perfectly on the
same bounded split therefore suggests that K2 rewards image-level visual anomaly cues more than
temporal surprise. The AUROC-1.0 non-LeWM rows still share F1 0.6667 and balanced accuracy 0.5
under the shared train-normal \texttt{p95} threshold rule, highlighting again that ranking
quality and threshold calibration are distinct. A promising next step is to inject glitch frames
into normal sequences to create synthetic temporal context; we leave that as future work.

\subsection{K3 Mechanistic Ablation (Honest Negative)}

Table~\ref{tab:k3-ablation-results} summarizes the controlled K3 validation-normal readout.
This is an honest negative mechanistic finding reported as a community-value contribution.

\input{tables/k3_ablation_results}

Across the six matched SIGReg-on versus SIGReg-off comparisons, the lower-is-better prediction
loss never improves when SIGReg is enabled. Real-action versus zero-action prediction-loss
deltas are also not directionally stable across seeds. This is an honest negative mechanistic
readout for the specific K3 training objective; it does not invalidate the detection-performance
results above, but it does indicate that the SIGReg and action-conditioning components of
LeWM do not provide measurable benefits at this scale and configuration.
